Designing a conversion-focused subscription screen that balances business goals, parental trust, and legal compliance — all on a single mobile screen.

My Role UX/UI Designer — solo project
Tools Figma · Adobe Photoshop · FigJam
Deliverables UI design · Interactive prototype · User flow
01

Context

The client is a mobile gaming studio behind a catalog of 20+ educational apps for children aged 3–8 — covering reading, math, music, and creative play. All apps are designed for screen time parents feel good about.

The task: design a paywall screen shown when a parent tries to access premium content. The screen needed to convert parents into subscribers while making them feel confident, not pressured, about the purchase.

I was the sole designer — responsible for research, UX logic, UI design, and the interactive Figma prototype.

Goals defined at the start

02

The problem

A paywall for a children's app has a core tension: the decision-maker is not the user. The child plays; the parent pays. Parents are interrupted mid-session, often skeptical, and need specific reassurances before entering their payment details.

Common conversion tactics — countdown timers, hidden cancellation, dark patterns — would backfire here. Parents of young children are acutely sensitive to feeling manipulated. The screen needed to do the opposite: build trust fast, show real value, feel transparent.

On top of that, app store guidelines and regional regulations require that subscription terms, cancellation policy, and legal documents are clearly accessible before purchase, without scrolling. Design and compliance had to be solved together.

03

The user

Primary persona

The Conscious Parent

Parent of a child aged 3–8. Values educational screen time. Concerned about data privacy. Makes purchase decisions quickly on mobile — often mid play-session.

  • Needs to instantly understand what they're paying for
  • Needs reassurance: safe, educational, worth the price
  • Needs confidence that child data is protected
  • Needs legal info visible before purchase — no surprises
  • Wants 1–2 taps to complete, not a multi-step flow

One key insight shaped the layout: parental guilt about screen time is real. A parent who believes the subscription delivers genuine educational value — not just entertainment — is far more likely to convert and stay subscribed.

This is why the screen leads with the game carousel and feature icons before the price. Value is established first; cost comes after.

Offline Mode received special visual emphasis. For parents who travel with children, the ability to play without internet is often the single most persuasive feature.

04

Process

I began by mapping the user flow — every path from seeing the paywall to completing or declining a purchase. The "No" path needed design attention too: declining shouldn't feel punitive, and the free tier should remain accessible without friction.

User flow — from paywall trigger to subscription or free-tier fallback

Decision 01

Lead with the game catalog, not the price

A scrolling carousel of 20+ game covers immediately shows the scope of what's included. Vibrant visuals reduce hesitation before the price appears.

Decision 02

Yearly plan pre-selected with a clear discount badge

Annual plan is highlighted from load with a –50% badge. Monthly exists as a low-commitment entry without undermining the annual value proposition.

Decision 03

Everything on one screen — no scrolling required

Features, pricing, CTA, cancellation notice, and legal links all fit in a single viewport. This satisfies both UX goals and compliance requirements simultaneously.

05

Final solution

The screen follows a clear visual hierarchy: value → features → price → action. Every element earns its place.

The carousel autoscrolls to show variety. The five benefit icons use color-coded iconography parents recognize instantly. Pricing cards are visually distinct — the yearly plan uses an orange border to signal recommendation without forcing it.

The CTA button always reflects the selected plan, reinforcing the parent's choice rather than pressuring them toward a default.

Prototype

Interactive walkthrough

The prototype demonstrates the full purchase flow: the game carousel opens immediately, the parent selects a plan and taps Unlock all 20+ apps, which triggers the native in-app purchase sheet with Face ID / Touch ID. Legal documents open as overlay screens without leaving the paywall. Closing the paywall returns the user to the game — no dead ends.

06

What I'd do differently

The biggest gap I'd close next time is the absence of real user testing. All decisions — the carousel lead, the pre-selected annual plan, the weight given to Offline Mode — are grounded in UX reasoning, but not validated with real parents. Even five moderated sessions would either confirm the hierarchy or reveal blind spots I couldn't see from a desk.

I'd also explore a free-trial variant of the CTA. A "Start 7-day free trial" framing consistently reduces abandonment on paywalls for family-focused apps. I built the current version around a direct purchase flow — testing it against a trial variant would be the obvious next experiment.

Finally, the plan-switching animation in the prototype could be more considered. It's functional right now, but a more deliberate micro-interaction — the price smoothly transitioning between states — would reinforce the quality signal the rest of the design works hard to establish.

07

Result

The project was delivered as a complete UI design and an interactive Figma prototype demonstrating both subscription states and the full purchase flow, including Face ID / Touch ID authentication steps.

The interactive prototype let the client test and present the flow to stakeholders without developer involvement, shortening the feedback cycle significantly. The design was approved without structural revisions.
