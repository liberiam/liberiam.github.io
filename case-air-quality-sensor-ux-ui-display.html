01

The task

The device has two embedded IPS displays — a 1.9″ vertical and a 1.8″ round. The task: design both screens to display air quality data (CO₂, PM2.5, PM10, TVOC, atmospheric pressure, humidity, temperature, lux) so users can read the current state at a glance, without opening the app.

The problem is constraint: a lot of data, very small screens. The layout needs to be intentional — the user must instantly know where each number lives and what it means.

Two screens: 1.9″ vertical (172×320 px) and 1.8″ round (360×360 px)

02

Design constraints

Embedded hardware UI is not like mobile design. The constraints are physical and non-negotiable.

01 · Hardware

Fixed pixel grid

172×320 px and 360×360 px — every layout decision is absolute. No fluid layouts, no breakpoints. Pixel-perfect or broken.

02 · Data density

8 sensors · 2 tiny screens

8 sensor readings need to fit across two displays without crowding. Priority determines placement: the three most critical indicators get the vertical screen; everything else goes on the round.

03 · Round format

Circle is not a rectangle

The 360×360 px round display clips corners. Content placed in the corners is lost. All secondary data had to be arranged in a circular-aware layout, centred and layered.

04 · Context

No interaction — ambient display

Users don't tap or swipe the device screen. It's always-on, always-readable. The display is ambient information, not an interface — closer to a clock face than a phone screen.

05 · Alert state

Status must change visually

When CO₂ or PM enters the danger zone, the display must communicate urgency without sound by default. The background colour of the affected zone shifts — turning the ambient screen into an active alert.

03

The solution

The vertical screen is divided into three equal horizontal zones: CO₂ on top, TVOC in the middle, PM at the bottom. These zones physically mirror the LED indicators on the device enclosure — so the display clarifies what each disc means. When a reading reaches danger level, the corresponding zone background turns red.

The round screen uses the circular format intentionally — like a clock face, made data-rich. Time occupies the centre at large size; secondary readings (lux, pressure, temperature, humidity) form a grid below; status icons (Wi-Fi, MQTT, sound, vibration) sit at the top arc. Date and day of week anchor the bottom.

Layout concept: 3-zone vertical screen + circular ambient display

04

Typography

Embedded display typography has different rules from screen design. The font must be readable at arm's length on a 1.9″ screen, recognisable instantly for both numbers and labels, and carry a visual language appropriate for a hi-tech sensor device — without looking like a cheap clock radio.

Primary — numbers & clock

DS-Digital Bold

Chosen for legibility at distance and a nod to the electronic clocks of the second half of the 20th century. The segment-display aesthetic creates instant familiarity — users know how to read it.

Secondary — labels & dates

Square lowercase letters complement DS-Digital without competing. The neat, geometric form reads clearly at small sizes — ideal for sensor labels, units, and date strings on a constrained display.

05

Visual language

The background uses a hi-tech theme — blue, red, and black with a geometric glass-effect pattern. Data colours are white and pale turquoise for normal state, shifting to red for danger alerts.

This is intentional: the device should feel like precision instrumentation, not a consumer gadget. The visual language borrows from industrial monitoring equipment and 1970s digital clocks — familiar enough to read instantly.

Background

Hi-tech geometric pattern, dark. Blue-black base with angular light refractions — creates depth without distracting from data.

Data colour

White and pale turquoise (#00E5FF range) for normal readings. High contrast against the dark background at any viewing angle.

Danger state

Affected zone background shifts to red. The danger value also renders in red. No sound required — the colour change is the alert.

Brightness

Adjusts automatically based on ambient lux sensor readings. Dim at night, bright in sunlight — always readable, never blinding.

All states: normal readings (left) and CO₂ 2502 ppm danger alert — red zone active (right)

06

Design decisions

01 · Vertical screen

Zones mirror the LED indicators

The three zones on the vertical screen (CO₂ · TVOC · PM) physically align with the three LED indicators on the enclosure. The screen clarifies what each indicator means — they work as a system, not independently.

02 · Round screen

Clock face as information architecture

Time at the centre is universally understood. This anchors the round display as "ambient" rather than "critical". Secondary data radiates outward — familiar clock logic applied to environmental data.

03 · Danger state

Colour replaces sound as primary alert

When CO₂ crosses 2500 ppm, the affected zone background turns red — not a pop-up, not a tone. The change is visible from across the room and doesn't disturb everyone in a quiet home at night.

04 · Typography

Two fonts, two roles, zero confusion

DS-Digital Bold for all numbers and clock. Iceland Regular for all labels and dates. Once the user learns this pattern (immediately, instinctively), they never confuse a value for a label again.

05 · Background

Hi-tech theme, not consumer gadget

The geometric glass-effect background signals precision instrumentation. It differentiates the device from cheap consumer sensors and reinforces the brand's positioning — a tool, not a toy.

06 · Data priority

Split by urgency, not by sensor type

Critical data (what can hurt you) goes on the always-prominent vertical screen. Ambient data (context) goes on the round screen. This is a design decision about attention hierarchy, not screen real estate.

07

Result & reflection

Final result: both displays on device — green normal state

Both screen designs were validated on the physical POC device. The vertical screen provides a clear display of the most important data (CO2, TVOC, PM). The mapping of zones and LED indicators was instantly understood by every test participant without any explanation. The round screen functions as a genuine ambient display — users stop consciously reading it and start simply knowing the time and state.

The article documenting this work was published in Medium Design Bootcamp and received positive reception from the design community.

Earlier user testing on the device displays would have been valuable. The zone-to-disc mapping felt obvious in design — but "obvious in Figma" and "obvious on a physical device at 2 metres" are different things. Even three people testing the physical POC would have validated or challenged the hierarchy before committing to the enclosure design.

The hi-tech background is a strong aesthetic choice — but it may date the product faster than a cleaner approach. A future version should explore whether a minimal, purely typographic display achieves the same readability with longer-lasting visual appeal.
