01

Context

A mobile studio producing educational audio content for children approached me to design their podcast app from scratch. The catalog featured story-driven series — Animal Tales & Trails, Imagination Station, Curiosity Clubhouse, Dreamland Stories — each produced specifically for the 4–9 age group.

The app needed to work in two modes: a child browsing independently (choosing what to listen to) and a child mid-episode (controlling playback). Both screens had to be usable without adult assistance and without reading ability.

I was the sole designer, responsible for UX research, information architecture, visual design, and the final Figma prototype.

02

The problem

Most podcast apps are designed for adults: small tap targets, text-heavy navigation, dense catalogues, and controls that assume reading ability and fine motor precision. Applied to a child audience, this creates three compounding problems:

Motor control
Imprecise finger control
Children aged 4–7 have significantly less precise finger control than adults. Standard 44pt tap targets produce constant misses and frustration.
Pre-literacy
Can't read yet
The core audience (4–6) cannot read. All navigation and content discovery must work through illustration, color, and simple iconography alone.
Unsupervised use
No adult in the room
Parents hand the phone to a child and leave. The interface must be self-explanatory — no one to ask for help when something goes wrong.

The design challenge was not aesthetic — it was structural: how do you build a functional, engaging audio app when your user can't read labels, can't reliably hit small targets, and is alone with the device?

03

The user

Primary user

The Young Listener

Child aged 4–9. Likely handed the phone by a parent. May not yet read. Responds to visual storytelling, characters, and bright illustration. Has short attention span and high curiosity.

  • Needs to find content by picture, not by title text
  • Needs large, obvious controls — no hunting for buttons
  • Needs clear feedback: is this playing? did my tap work?
  • Wants engaging, character-driven visuals that match the audio
  • Needs to return to "playing now" easily if they wander off
Secondary user

The Permitting Parent

Sets up the app, hands it to the child. Not actively engaged but cares about safety — no unexpected purchases, no adult content pathways, no addictive dark patterns.

  • Needs the app to be self-sufficient — no interruptions for help
  • Needs content that is clearly child-safe and educational
  • Needs no dark patterns — no accidental subscriptions
  • Appreciates simple navigation the child can figure out alone

04

Process

I began by establishing a set of non-negotiable design principles specific to the child audience — these acted as constraints throughout every design decision that followed.

Visual first

No text-only navigation. Every screen, podcast, and action must be identifiable through illustration or iconography. Labels exist as reinforcement, not as the primary signal.

Generous targets

Minimum tap target: 60×60pt. The play/pause button is the largest element on the player screen. Prev/Next are large enough to hit while distracted.

Clear feedback

Every tap produces visible change. The play button animates between states. The progress bar moves. There is never a moment where the child can't tell if their action registered.

No dead ends

The child can always get back. A persistent bottom navigation means Home and "Now Playing" are always one tap away from any screen.

Warm palette

Soft, high-contrast colours — not harsh primaries. The blue/peach/cream palette feels safe and cheerful without being overstimulating. Actionable elements (orange) are visually distinct from content.

Decision 01

Illustration as the primary navigation signal

Each podcast has a unique character illustration. The child picks "the boy with the fox" — not "Animal Tales & Trails". Artwork does the work text can't.

Decision 02

Player-first layout on the player screen

The cover illustration fills 55% of the screen height. The child always knows what they're listening to, even before reading ability develops.

Decision 03

5 controls max, all oversized

Download · Prev · Play/Pause · Next · Sound. The play button is 72pt — three times standard size. The others stay large enough to hit while distracted.

05

Final solution

The app resolves into two primary screens with a persistent bottom navigation connecting them.

The Player screen is almost entirely occupied by the episode illustration — the child sees the world of the story they're in. Below it: episode title, a gentle description, a progress bar, and the five large controls. Nothing competes for attention with the artwork.

The Home screen shows a featured episode illustration at the top (again, full-bleed, illustration-first), then a scrollable list of podcasts with thumbnail artwork, title, and duration. No text-only rows — every item has a face to recognise.

06

What I'd do differently

The most significant gap in this project was the absence of direct observation with children. Every design principle I applied — large targets, illustration-first, minimal controls — is grounded in established child HCI research, but I didn't get to watch a 5-year-old actually use the prototype. Even two hours of observation sessions would have surfaced edge cases I can't predict from a desk: which icon confuses them, where they look first, what they do when lost.

I'd also push harder on the onboarding moment. The current design assumes the child is handed the app already set up. A short, wordless animated intro — showing a finger tapping the cover, then the player appearing — would dramatically reduce first-session friction.

Finally, I'd explore audio feedback as a design layer. A gentle sound when the play button activates, a different tone when Prev/Next taps register — audio confirmation removes the need to look at the screen to know if the tap worked, which matters enormously for this age group.

07

Result

The project delivered a complete UI design for the two primary app screens and a Figma prototype demonstrating the core listening flow. The visual language — soft illustration, oversized controls, warm palette — tested positively with the client as a clear departure from generic audio app templates.

The design system established here — large targets, illustration-as-navigation, persistent bottom nav — is reusable across future screens as the app catalog grows.
