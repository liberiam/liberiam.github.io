01

Ecosystem design

Before the pitch deck, the ecosystem itself needed to be designed as a coherent system — not four separate products, but one integrated solution with clear roles for each component. The design challenge: make a complex hardware + SaaS + automation system feel simple and inevitable to an investor who sees it for the first time.

The three-pillar framework — Monitor · Control · Safety — was the answer. Every product in the line and every feature in the SaaS maps to one of these pillars. This framework became the spine of the pitch deck, the landing page, and the brand.

01
Monitor
Real-time CO₂, PM2.5, VOC, NOx, temp, humidity, lux. 8 sensors, on-device display, mobile app.
02
Control
Unified hub via mobile app or smart home. Apple Home, Google Home, Alexa, Tuya, Home Assistant.
03
Safety
Automatic risk elimination. Auto Drive opens windows. Climate control triggers. Threshold alerts.

04

Pitch deck

I designed the full investor pitch deck — all 13 slides. The CEO provided financial model, unit economics, roadmap, and team bios. My job: translate those numbers into a visual narrative that communicates the business opportunity clearly and memorably.

The deck works on two levels simultaneously: as a visual story (why this problem, why this product, why this team, why now) and as a financial document (TAM/SAM/SOM, LTV breakdown, gross margins, hardware unit economics, SaaS scaling tables, use of funds). Every slide balances both without making either feel like it's competing.

The visual system: clean backgrounds, consistent type hierarchy, structured data tables with colour-coded metrics, product renders used as anchoring visuals throughout. The same visual language extends into the one-pager, brand positioning document, and competitive analysis — a coherent investor-facing design system.

05

What I'd do differently

Working as the sole designer across a product this wide — hardware, software, brand, investor materials, and a live website — meant making hundreds of decisions without the feedback loops a typical design process provides.

A unified design token system from day one. Brand, device UI, landing page, and pitch deck developed somewhat in parallel. The colour system evolved as the zone palette was refined — which meant reworking materials. A single source of truth for tokens from the start would have saved significant rework.

Dedicated UX sprints for the SaaS app. Designing hardware, brand, and software simultaneously meant the mobile app received less systematic attention in the early phases. A dedicated sprint would have produced a more coherent onboarding and daily-use flow.

Separate design review cycles for investor materials. The pitch deck was reviewed by the founding team — but not by anyone outside it. An external reviewer with investor experience would have caught assumptions that felt obvious to us but needed more context for a first-time reader.
